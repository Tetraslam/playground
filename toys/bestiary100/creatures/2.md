## Anatomy

A flat, leaf-shaped body about the size of a human hand, sheathed in a living shell of iron-sulfide scales it precipitates from vent water. Beneath: six vestigial legs and a broad, frilled "anode lobe" at the head end densely packed with conductive filaments that generate a weak voltage across the body — hot side positive, cool side negative — as sulfide-rich water flows over it. No eyes; the lobe functions as a coarse electric field sensor for navigation in the dark.

## Behavior

It crawls the mineralized rim of active fissures, grazing the sulfur-bearing crust itself, dissolving it with acid exuded from its foot and re-plating the metals onto its shell as it grows. When the fissure it lives on cools or shifts, it migrates by walking slowly across the deep floor toward the next thermal plume, following the faint electric signature of heated water; each individual may outlive three or four vents in its century-long span. Solitary and aggressively territorial: two *Chemolectrix* meeting on the same rim discharge their voltage into each other until one's shell cracks and is consumed.

## Myth

Vent-dwelling cultures prize intact shells as lanterns — they faintly glow from residual thermoelectric current for years after the animal dies. They are believed to hold the last warmth of a vent that has gone cold.
