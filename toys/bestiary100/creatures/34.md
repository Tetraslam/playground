## Anatomy

A streamlined hull the length of a rowboat, hydrofoil-rigid along the spine but with a ventral throat-sac of distensible membrane that can balloon to three times the resting volume. The anterior end is a rigid cartilaginous funnel ringed with keratinous gill-combs; the posterior tapers to two cleft exhaust vents. Two long swept gliding wings — no flapping musculature, only articulation at the root — hold it in updrafts. It has no alighting legs, no closing mouth, and no lungs in the terrestrial sense: the entire body is a duct, and respiration is combustion-free ramjet flow.

## Behavior

Aerochone must keep moving or suffocate. It circles the open air between landmasses in slow glides, then stoops into a dive to ram thin Aether through its funnel; the gill-combs strip out aerial plankton, mist-borne larvae, and smaller sailers, while the passing air oxygenates a countercurrent blood-sheet lining the duct and provides thrust out the vents. It sleeps in a descending spiral, losing altitude by the meter per minute, waking to dive and climb again — it has never in its life touched a surface. Reproduction is by extrusion: the female electrolyses water-vapor in her gizzard into hydrogen and seeds a buoyant egg-sac out the vent; the sac rises into the Rime, freezes, and cracks open weeks later in high wind, scattering gliding juveniles that migrate down the air column over years.

## Myth

Aether-crossers say a circling Aerochone seen at dusk is the safest sign there is — it means the updraft is true and the air between landmasses is fat with life. To see one fall, though, is an ill omen: the folk believe a Perpetual Draft only stops when the world itself forgets how to breathe, and that the first one to land will be the last thing the Drift ever does.
