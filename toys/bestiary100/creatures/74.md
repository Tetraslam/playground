## Anatomy

A vertical rod the length of a forearm, standing upright in a fissure crack with its hot foot buried in superheated brine and its cold crown in ambient deep water. The body wall is a living thermopile: thousands of stacked junctions between biomineralized bismuth telluride and a copper-protein alloy, each junction a single cell thick, generating current across the 200°C gradient that runs its whole length. Around this core the stave electroplates a tapering casing of iron sulfide, deposited layer by layer from the plume itself, so an old stave looks like a blackened chimney rising from the crack. No mouth, no gut; a frilled copper-colored crown at the top vents oxygen split from water, the hydrogen jetted separately out a basal pore.

## Behavior

The stave fixes carbon by driving the Calvin cycle backward with its own current, reducing dissolved CO2 and sulfide pulled across the casing wall — it eats the fissure's chemistry, not its creatures. It is effectively sessile for decades, but when a fissure cools or silts shut the gradient collapses and the stave fires its basal pore: a sustained hydrogen jet that lifts it bodily up into the dark current, crown trailing, to tumble until it strikes a new hot crack and re-anchors. The same discharge, fired laterally, stuns the small vent-shrimp that settle on its casing; the stunned prey simply rots against the sulfide wall and is absorbed through it, a supplement the stave tolerates but does not require. Staves reproduce by basal budding: a daughter rod fissions off already embedded in the parent's casing, splits away during a jet-launch, and tumbles to its own crack.

## Myth

Vent-divers say the staves are the deep's lightning rods, bleeding off the world's buried heat one volt at a time so the Drift doesn't burst. A diver who touches a living crown comes back "copper-tongued" — numb, tasting metal for a week — which is taken as proof they spoke to the dark and the dark answered.
