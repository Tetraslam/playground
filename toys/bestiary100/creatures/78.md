## Anatomy

A soft, slate-dark ovoid body the length of a thumb, creeping on a ciliated ventral sole, carrying on its back a single faceted quartz lens it has secreted and optically ground over its lifetime — a biomineral prism half its own mass, grown layer by layer from dissolved glass-waste silica. Beneath the lens lies a translucent sac of phototrophic endosymbionts that power the animal; the lens is not an eye but a feedhorn, concentrating the Waste's refracted glare onto the sac. There is no head, no mouth, no anus — only the sole, the sac, and the lens, which the animal can tilt and lower against the desert floor on a muscular pedicel.

## Behavior

A colony of fifty to three hundred moves in loose single file across the brightest refraction fields, each individual periodically lowering its lens to the glass surface and holding still while focused sun etches a microscopic diffraction grating into the substrate — a permanent line of parallel grooves a few microns deep. The gratings are read by passing conspecifics through the polarization spectrum they scatter: specific groove spacings encode thermal refuges, shadow-predator warnings, and mating stations, so the Glass Wastes are, to those who can read them, a single continuous text written across thousands of square kilometers. When a shadow crosses its lens the scribe retracts the pedicel in a reflexive snap, tucks the prism flat, and goes inert for hours; the etched record is the colony's only durable memory, since no individual lives longer than two seasons.

## Myth

Wastes-runners say the desert is not empty but *written*, and that a traveler who learns to read the gratings will never die of exposure — every refuge, every water-sheen, every safe path is already inscribed by the scribes for those patient enough to decode the light. A lens found detached from its dead scribe is kept as a talisman and called a "borrowed eye"; looking through it at noon is said to show not the Waste ahead but the Waste as it was the day the scribe fell.
