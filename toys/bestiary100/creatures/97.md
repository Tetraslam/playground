## Anatomy

Osteosuture has no body of its own in any ordinary sense — it is a living film of acicular apatite crystals and acid-secreting cells that exists only *inside* the fossil bone of other, long-dead Drift organisms. A single individual is a threadbare network of hollow filaments, each thinner than a hair, that threads itself along the Haversian canals and lacunae of a fossilized skeleton, dissolving calcium phosphate ahead of itself and re-depositing it behind, so the creature advances through solid bone the way a root advances through soil — never leaving a void, only a faintly re-crystallized wake. Its "shape" is therefore the shape of whatever dead thing it inhabits: a rib, a skull, a joint, faithfully followed.

## Behavior

It feeds on the residual organics locked in fossil matrix — the ghost-collagen and lipid films that even mineralized bone retains after millions of years, which no surface detritivore can reach. A filament advances a few centimeters a year, etching as it goes, and where two fossils are pressed together in the strata an Osteosuture will slowly bridge from one into the next, re-threading the contact. Crucially, the re-deposited apatite behind it is not random: the creature re-crystallizes in the *grain* of whatever it passed through, preserving and even sharpening the fossil's internal structure. An Osteosuture that has lived a century has, in effect, memorized every skeleton it crossed — its trailing wake is a finer fossil record than the rock it grew in. It breeds by fracture: when badland erosion splits a fossil, spore-filaments along the fresh face dessicate and scatter, germinating only on contact with new bone.

## Myth

Bone-Field scavengers say the deepest fossils are not dead but *overwritten* — that the Osteosuture does not preserve the past but is slowly re-writing it, and that the Drift's oldest strata now record creatures that never lived, drawn by the suture from some skeleton it has not yet reached.
