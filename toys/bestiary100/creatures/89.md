## Anatomy

Cryostega is a colonial organism that grows as a single hexagonal lattice of living ice across the undersides of rime-clouds, anywhere from twenty to three hundred meters across but never more than a hand's-breadth thick. Each cell of the lattice is a double-walled prism: an outer shell of clear hoarfrost, and an inner film of ammonia-water cytoplasm laced with antifreeze proteins that flows along grain boundaries like sap, keeping the lattice alive at -60°. The ventral face of every cell is ground into a shallow lens, so the whole canopy is a mosaic of ice optics — and beneath each lens sits a knot of cryo-active gland cells that can, in a millisecond, seed nucleation across a patch of sky the size of a ship's sail.

## Behavior

It hunts by waiting. When something warm crosses beneath the canopy, its heat bends the nearest lenses out of focus; the colony reads the distortion as a map of the body and fires only the cells whose focal point coincides with living tissue, freezing the moisture in the prey's own breath and plumage onto a thousand threads at once and hauling it upward into the lattice, where enzymes that only work below -40° dissolve it over the course of weeks. Between kills it trickle-feeds on the organic films that condense out of the upper atmosphere onto its upper face, photo-oxidizing them through the ice. Storms tear it apart — and that is how it breeds: each shard carries a spore node, and any fragment that lands on a new rime-cloud nucleates a fresh colony oriented to the local wind.

## Myth

Aether-crossers call a Cryostega canopy a "wrong-glint," because in the right slant of light its thousand lenses throw a single perfect hexagonal moiré that no natural rime ever makes. They will fly days out of their way to avoid one, insisting the colony does not kill you — it *remembers* you, and that a face frozen into the lattice a century ago can still be read, weeping, if you chip it out.
