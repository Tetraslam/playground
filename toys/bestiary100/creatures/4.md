## Anatomy

A rigid vertical column, 30–80 cm tall, clamped across the thermal boundary layer of a vent orifice: its base sits in 320°C brine, its crown in 4°C abyssal water. The body is a stack of hundreds of alternating rings — pyrite-impregnated chitin (the hot, n-type layers) and sulfide glass (the cold, p-type layers) — functioning as a biological Peltier cell that sustains a steady ~0.4V Seebeck current across its length. There is no mouth, gut, or gonad in the metazoan sense; metabolism is entirely electrochemical, the current driving the reduction of CO₂ and H₂S into fixed carbon at internal catalytic plates.

## Behavior

Sessile for its adult life. It grows by accreting new rings at the midline, each one precipitated from the mineral plume it straddles, so an individual's age is readable in its ring count like a core sample. Reproduction is fission: once a column exceeds ~90 rings it splits longitudinally, and the two half-columns roll inward into cylinders, detach, and ride vent plumes to colonize new fissures. A laddermouth displaced from its gradient dies in minutes — its current collapses and the rings delaminate.

## Myth

Vent-dwelling cultures regard a tall, many-ringed laddermouth as a "stacked year" and will not harvest one, believing each ring holds a memory of the deep that the world-tree is still metabolizing.
