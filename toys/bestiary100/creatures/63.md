## Anatomy

A single dendritic ice crystal the span of a forearm, anchored by a protein plug to ancient pack-ice on the Rime floor — but the crystal is *inhabited*. A hair-thin lattice of fold-switching glycoprotein threads through the ice at molecular spacing, deciding where each branch advances and where growth halts. There is no tissue, no cavity, no gut: the organism is the interface between vapor and solid, and its metabolism runs on the latent heat released at every depositing tip, which drives conformational flips in the protein lattice the way ATP drives a kinase. The whole body is glass-clear, faintly bluish, unreadable as life until wind shifts and the dendrites begin, audibly, to tick.

## Behavior

Sessile and upward-growing, the loom orients its branches into the prevailing vapor stream, harvesting water and the trace organics wind-borne from lower drifts; a mature crystal moves no mass, only reshapes itself, sublimating one edge while depositing another so the body migrates across the ice over weeks without ever lifting. Threat response is abscission: a flood of antifreeze protein into a chosen segment melts it free within seconds, dropping a perfect decoy crystal for the predator to shatter while the parent reseals and regrows. At senescence the lattice deliberately faults along every grain boundary, shattering the crystal into hundreds of seed shards scattered on the wind, each one nucleating a new loom where it freezes fast to old ice.

## Myth

Rime-pilots say the ticking of a loom in a shifting wind is the sound of the world deciding whether to keep its shape. A shard fallen on bare skin is called a "cold vow" — it will not melt, only sink in, and the bearer is said to owe the ice a return.
