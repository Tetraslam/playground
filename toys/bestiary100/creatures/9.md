## Anatomy

Resonotheca is not one animal but a syncytial lattice, a single shared cytoplasm extending through dozens of hollow aragonite-silica tubes it secretes over a thermal fissure. The tubes vary in bore from a hair's width to a fist, each length tuned like an organ pipe to a specific resonant frequency. There is no head, no gut, no symmetry; the organism is a cathedral of flues, its living matter lining the interior walls as a ciliated film one cell thick.

## Behavior

It feeds by shaping the lattice into turbulence traps: vent updraft carries shrimp, gnathids, and drifting motes into the tubes, where cilia walk them to digestive pockets that bud and resorb as needed. Because each tube is tuned, Resonotheca continuously "hears" the vent's pressure signature through sympathetic vibration; when a tube's tone shifts, it knows something large is moving upstream. When a rival colony encroaches or a predator attacks, it slams shut a ring of apertures in sequence, producing a subsonic knell that stuns vent-fauna and cracks the mineral footing of competitors. Reproduction is by fragmentation: a tube sheared off in a quake drifts to a new fissure and, if it lands heat-side-up, grows a fresh lattice.

## Myth

Vent-divers claim the knell of a large Resonotheca is the world groaning at its hinges, and that a colony which has rung continuously for a century can predict eruptions days in advance. Some leave offerings of scrap metal at the fissure mouth, believing the lattice incorporates the metal into its tubes and remembers the giver.
