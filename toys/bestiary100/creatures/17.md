## Anatomy

A flat radiating sheet of branching capillaries with no body, no head, no skin — an exposed circulatory system laid bare across the surface of Mire water. Each strand is a one-cell-thick tube driving decay-tea upward by ciliary pump; dissolved organics cross the wall directly, no gut required. The hemolymph is wine-dark, stained by anoxic symbionts that fix sulfur from the rot below, so a mature weft reads from above as a burgundy delta fanning across black water.

## Behavior

It grows radially from a founding knot, fanning across still water and retracting nightly into a tight fist to evade grazers. Reproduction is traumatic and accidental: predators tear fragments loose, and any piece wider than a finger regrows a complete weft within a season by re-branching from the torn edge. Where two wefts meet they graft along the seam and share flow, producing chimera-sheets that can cover hectares and fuse the bloodlines of strangers.

## Myth

Mire-folk count crossing a graft-chimera as a marriage — two bloods now one, the travelers bound. A weft crossed seven times is called an elder, and is never stepped on.
