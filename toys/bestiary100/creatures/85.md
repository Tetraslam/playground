## Anatomy

Cyclotherma duplex is a sessile vertical tube, one to two meters long, anchored at its base to the lip of a hydrothermal chimney so that its lower third sits in 300°C sulfide plume and its upper two-thirds extend into the 3°C abyssal water above. There is no heart. The body cavity is a sealed chamber holding a dense working gas (mostly CO₂ with trace hydrogen sulfide), and a muscular tissue mass — the displacer — shuttles this gas pocket between the hot foot and the cold crown every few seconds. The resulting pressure swings pump hemolymph through a ring of check-valved channels, so circulation is a living Stirling cycle: the vent's heat is the only thing that ever moves its blood. The outer wall is sheathed in a black pyrite-pyrrhotite lattice — a natural thermocouple — that harvests the same gradient as a low-voltage current running the animal's sparse nervous tissue. It has no gut; a mat of chemolithoautotrophic archaea lines the hot foot, fixing sulfide into sugar that the hemolymph carries up the tube.

## Behavior

It never moves after settlement. A larva is a buoyant ciliated speck riding thermal plumes until it contacts a fresh chimney orifice, where it cements, elongates, and begins cycling within hours. Growth adds length only at the crown, pushing the cold end ever higher into colder water, which steepens the gradient and quickens the cycle — old specimens throb audibly. Reproduction is synchronized to vent surges: when the plume temperature spikes, the crown releases thousands of larvae in a single exhalation, riding the same updraft that seeded it. If the chimney cools or sinter-over buries the foot, the displacer stalls within minutes and the animal dies in place, leaving a perfect pyritized tube-cast in the dead rock.

## Myth

Vent-divers call Cyclotherma "the world's bellows" and time their descents by its throb, which they swear syncs across every chimney in a field like a single slow heartbeat. A vent that goes silent is considered dead in truth, no matter how hot it still burns — and divers claim that a chimney hosting many tightly-packed tubes will keep a dying plume alive a little longer, as if the animals were pumping heat back into the rock out of loyalty.
