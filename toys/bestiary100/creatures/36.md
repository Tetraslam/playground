## Anatomy

A rigid triangular sail, thirty centimeters on a side, secreted as a biological aerogel — a freeze-died lattice of protein and atmospheric ice so porous it is mostly air, lighter than the thin stratosphere it rides. A dense crystalline keel of re-deposited rime hangs below as ballast and houses the only soft tissue: a thumb-sized ganglion, two polarization-sensitive ocelli, and a row of sublimation pits along the sail's trailing edge. The sail itself is the metabolism: laced with a UV-tolerant pigment that splits trace CO₂ and methane directly off punishing high-altitude ultraviolet, fixing carbon into more aerogel. The airframe is alive, disposable, and continuously rebuilt.

## Behavior

It never lands. Steering is by controlled self-consumption — the ganglion warms a sublimation pit, the adjacent sail panel evaporates asymmetrically, the airfoil tilts, and the caravel tacks across a current it cannot otherwise propel itself through. It reads the sky's polarization through its ocelli and holds a compass course for days, trimming sail as the wind veers. To reproduce it grows a mirror-second sail fused along the keel; when the pair's combined lift exceeds the ballast they cleave apart at a grain boundary and the new caravel inherits half the keel. A dead caravel is one that has eaten more sail than it can regrow — it sublimates whole, leaving only the keel to fall.

## Myth

High-altitude voyagers read the caravels as living weather-vanes: a fleet all pointing one azimuth means a current the landmass will ride within the week. A sky full of still caravels — none tacking, none sublimating — is the worst omen in the Drift, said to precede a "calmfall," when the upper winds die and suspended land begins, imperceptibly, to sink.
