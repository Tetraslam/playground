## Anatomy

A low, mineral tortoise-analogue the width of a barrel, built on a chitinous underframe carrying six stubby, rock-gripping legs and a forward acid-radula that dissolves fossil bone in situ. Its metabolism is chemoautotrophic: it oxidizes pyrite inclusions common in fossil beds and fixes the released CO2, while the dissolved calcium phosphate is not consumed for energy but re-precipitated onto a dorsal scaffold as a slowly accreting banded spire of carbonate-apatite. The spire is the creature's record — each band is a different fossil's mineral signature, laid down in the order eaten, so a mature Osteocairn carries a literal stratigraphic core-sample of every bone-bed it has crossed.

## Behavior

It is a migratory mineral-gnawer, moving only meters a day along exposed fossil horizons, lowering its radula to a seam and dissolving a trough over weeks. The spire grows faster than the body can carry it; when the center of mass shifts past recovery the Osteocairn topples sideways, the spire shatters, and any fragment bearing a fragment of the underframe and a viable acid-plate buds a new individual that begins its own spire from the broken base. Thus reproduction is spire-fission, and a shatter-field of fallen spires is both a graveyard and a nursery. It shares the badlands with Taphocastus but ignores it: one eats the fossil's mold, the other eats the fossil itself, and they pass in the dark without quarrel.

## Myth

Bone-Field prospectors call them "votive cairns" and follow live ones as divining rods — the band-colors of a walking spire betray which strata run fossil-rich below, saving months of test-pitting. A toppled spire is a free survey core, and mapping clans will kill an Osteocairn on purpose to read its record. They say the creature is paying back the bones it borrows, one layer at a time, and that when the last fossil is dissolved the Drift will end — so the kindest act is to let one walk.
