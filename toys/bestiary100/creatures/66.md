## Anatomy

A branching filamentous lattice of biogenic pyrite and chalcopyrite, one to three meters long, secreted as a living film of archaea-like cells around a hollow conductive core. It spans a thermal gradient — its hot foot anchored in 320°C brine at a smoker orifice, its cold apex fanned out into 4°C abyssal water — and the temperature differential across its own body drives a continuous thermoelectric current (the Seebeck effect, the same physics that powers a deep-space RTG). There is no gut, no heart, no chemistry of respiration as we know it: the lattice *is* the metabolism, the nervous system, and the skeleton, all one structure. The cold apex bears a faint electrosensory halo of charged filaments; the hot foot is a porous plaque that plates dissolved metals out of the vent fluid wherever current concentrates.

## Behavior

Galvanothrix is effectively sessile but inches along fissure walls over months by dissolving its trailing anchor and reprecipitating metal at the leading edge — a slow treadmill that always crawls toward a steeper gradient. It does not hunt, but any vent fauna that brushes the lattice is seized by an electroplating current: metals from the brine flash-coat the intruder's chitin, immobilizing it within seconds, and the coating is then absorbed over days by the foot film. Reproduction is by thermal snap: a quake or geyser shears a fragment, which drifts in the plume until it lodges across a fresh gradient and re-roots; a lone strand can seed an entire chimney wall within a decade. The current never stops while the gradient holds — a Galvanothrix severed from its heat source goes dark, and never relights.

## Myth

Vent-dwellers call it "the wire that drinks the deep" and harvest shed lattice fragments as the only native source of worked metal on the Drift — but they will not cut a living strand, believing each one carries a single continuous thought that began when the fissure opened and ends only when the heat dies.
