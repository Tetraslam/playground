## Anatomy

A Holostrath is a single translucent sheet of silicified aerogel tissue, three to five meters across but barely a millimeter thick, buoyed by trapped low-density gas and ridden on thermal updrafts. Its ventral face is etched with millions of parallel calcite spicules set at sub-micron spacings — a living diffraction grating — and beneath the grating, embedded in the tissue, run ten thousand rows of photosynthetic symbiont mats, each row tuned to a different narrow band of the luminous atmosphere's spectrum. Microscopic ciliary muscles flex the grating's spacing by fractions of a nanometre, steering which wavelengths focus onto which row; the sheet is at once a solar collector, a spectroscope, and its own nervous readout, seeing the sky as a striped field of color rather than a brightness. There is no head, no gut, no symmetry axis — the animal is the membrane.

## Behavior

It drifts the open Aether and migrates vertically each day, sinking at dusk into the dense lower glow where reds and far-reds dominate, rising at noon into the thin upper violet, flexing its grating to hold every symbiont row at saturation. It has no predator defense but its own fragility: a striking raptor or a landmass's edge ruptures the sheet, and the torn strip — a "ribbon" — inflates, catches an updraft, and if it drifts into a spectral storm (the rare seasonal days when the atmosphere luminesces in anomalous bands, including a deep ultraviolet the calm sky never produces), the ribbon's dormant UV-symbiont row bootstraps and a new Holostrath nucleates around it. Most ribbons fall into the Mire and rot; only storms make more. Between storms a Holostrath is effectively immortal, healing its tears by re-growing grating across the gap, each patch bearing the spectral fingerprint of the day it was laid down.

## Myth

Aether-crossers read a Holostrath overhead as a compass: its grating throws a faint prismatic streak across the sheet that shifts with the day's dominant wavelength, and a sheet whose streak runs "wrong" is said to mark where the Drift's own light is sickening. Sailors who pass beneath one during a spectral storm swear they hear it — a faint tonal hum they insist is the sheet singing in the wavelengths their eyes cannot reach.
