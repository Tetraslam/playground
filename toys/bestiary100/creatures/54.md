## Anatomy

A two-to-three-meter chitinous mast rooted in the upper frost, crowned with terraces of clear biogenic ice the animal grows itself by secreting nucleator proteins along controlled growth fronts and dissolving them back with a cryoprotectant where it wants absence. The mast is not round but a fluted column of piezoelectric chitin — when the ice-laden crown flexes in the high wind, the column generates charge along its flutes, and that charge is the only metabolism the animal has: no gut, no gut analog, only an electroactive lattice of cells that route current to the growth tips and to the fine whistle-edges it carves into each terrace. Color is the color of the ice it has grown — at rest a faint blue-green, in hard wind opalescent with internal fractures.

## Behavior

Cymatocrista stands fixed in the Rime and harvests the wind. Each terrace is shaped as a tuned edge; air crossing it sheds vortices at a frequency the animal has grown itself to produce, so a single mast hums a sustained chord whose notes shift as the wind shifts. The charge from the flexing column powers slow remodeling of the terraces — adding ice on the windward face, ablating on the lee — so the instrument drifts in pitch over weeks. It feeds on the same aerosol organics that condense in any Rime ice, but only as they are rime-deposited onto its own growing terraces; it cannot forage. Reproduction is calving: a lower terrace grows heavy, the mast constricts its chitin at the joint until the fracture is piezoelectrically charged, and the slab drops. If it lands ice-face-up on stable frost it germinates a new mast; if it falls through into warmer air it sublimes in minutes and is gone.

## Myth

Skyfarers cross the Rime by ear, not by eye: the chorus of Cymatocrista is the compass that sings, and a stretch of upper air gone silent is read as the ice dying from beneath — a warning to descend no further. A mast found standing but voiceless is called a *held breath*, and is left untouched, the belief being it is gathering the wind to carry a message for someone who never came back for it.
