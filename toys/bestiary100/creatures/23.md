## Anatomy

Each zooid is a fist-sized gas-bladdered sphere of translucent chitin, studded with photoreceptive chromatophores and trailing a silken tether twice its own length. Zooids knit tethers into a living gill-net — a single mature colony spanning forty to sixty meters of open sky, buoyed by metabolically-tuned lifting gas heavier than nothing but lighter than the luminous medium around it. There is no central body: the colony is a flat drifting sheet, its upper face a phototrophic membrane that drinks the aether's ambient glow, its lower face a charged mucus film that snares windborne organics and ionic dust.

## Behavior

Lumenskein ride the slow convection cells between landmasses and never touch solid ground across their entire lives. They steer weakly, inflating or venting individual bladders to tilt the sheet and catch a different stratum of current. Reproduction is fragmentation — storm shear tears a net, and each fragment buds new zooids outward from its torn edge, so a single colony is a clone-line that may have been dividing since before any current settlement was founded. Colonies that drift into denser air over the Canopy bloom visibly brighter, then dim and thin as they rise again.

## Myth

Aether-navigators hold that the glow of the open sky is not the Drift's breath but the assembled light of countless lumenskein, layered stratum on stratum out to the horizon. A sky gone dark in a corridor is read as a migration — the skeins have pulled up to higher air — and any ship setting out under a darkened sky is said to be sailing under a sentence, for the glow is what keeps the higher storms from descending.
