## Anatomy

A tapering column of precipitated iron-sulfide and chitin, two meters tall, shaped like a miniature hydrothermal chimney and indistinguishable from one at a distance. The outer wall is a porous mineral exoskeleton secreted by a basal ring of tissue; the hollow core channels vent water upward by thermal draft, and the upper third flares into a hooded mouth ringed with inward-pointing crystalline spines. The inner lining is the only living tissue — heat-sensing, iridescent black, dotted with chemosynthetic symbionts. There are no eyes; the world is read through temperature gradients across flank pores and dissolved-gas chemosensors.

## Behavior

Pyrostega plants on an active fissure and lets its own thermal plume merge with the vent's, becoming invisible to the thermotactic vent-fauna it hunts: prey drawn up the draft are caught on the spine ring and slowly dissolved. When a fissure cools, it seals both ends, holding hot water in the core as a thermal battery, and walks on its contractile basal ring at a few centimeters an hour toward a hotter reading, navigating by differential heat-sensing between opposite pores. Reproduction is by lateral budding — a daughter chimney grows off the base, shares the parent's plume until its mineral wall hardens, then snaps free in a vent tremor and rolls to a new fissure.

## Myth

Vent-dwellers call it "the false hearth" — a creature you cannot tell from the vent it sits on, said to be the vent dreaming it had a body. The oldest Pyrostega, they say, are not animals at all but vents that learned to walk; and one calm night they will all stand up at once and leave, and the deep Drift will go cold.
