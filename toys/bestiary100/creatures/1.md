## Anatomy

The Bismuth Flue is a spiraling stack of fifty to three hundred bismuth-telluride crystal lamellae, each a translucent wafer the size of a spread hand, grown in a slow logarithmic coil around a hollow central lumen. Between the lamellae lies a living film of sulfur-reducing archaea in a mercury-rich brine; the creature's only "tissue" is the iridescent membrane that secretes and indexes the crystals. It has no mouth, no eyes, no nervous system — navigation is by a thermoelectric sense: the ΔT across each lamella generates a tiny current, and the stack reads the gradient field as a map of the fissure around it.

## Behavior

It creeps along Vent fissure walls at roughly a meter per decade, electrolyzing metal sulfides out of the rock and leaving a calcified, glassy trail behind it. When two Flues meet they pause, exchange lamellae through their open lumens, and separate — a gene-shuffle with no mating. Reproduction is by fracture: a mature lamella shears off in a tremor, drifts down a convection plume, and begins seeding its own stack wherever the thermal gradient exceeds 80 K m⁻¹. A Flue can live for millennia; dead stacks persist as iridescent spiral chimneys that later colonists build upon.

## Myth

Vent-folk say each Flue is a page being written by the world's heat, and that the oldest stacks — half a canyon tall — are the unread autobiography of the Drift's core. Divers who steal a lamella report dreams in a language of pure temperature.
