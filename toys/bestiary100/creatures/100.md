## Anatomy

A Cymesail is a flat rhombic membrane half a meter across, rigged on a flexible chitin spar and flown like a kite above the world-tree's crown on a single living tether — a vascular umbilicus up to thirty meters long that roots directly into the tree's phloem. The membrane's dorsal face is a velvety mat of canopy-specific cyanobacterial symbionts tuned to the Drift's luminous spectrum; its ventral face is translucent, showing the slow pulse of sap drawn *down* the tether. There is no head, no mouth, no gut — the animal is a sail with a vein, and its entire body plan is a negotiation between two flows: sunlight falling on its back and sap sinking through its stalk.

## Behavior

By day it pays out tether and climbs the updraft above the crown, holding its mat broadside to the light; the sugars it fixes drain down the umbilicus into the tree's phloem, and in exchange the tree's deep-mined ions rise back up, so a hosted Cymesail is a leaf the tree did not have to grow. At dusk it winches in, furls the spar against a bud-scale, and sleeps flush to the bark. When a host tree senesces or the updraft fails, the tether self-severs and the sail glides on the luminous sea to a neighboring crown, re-rooting in a single night. Reproduction is budding: once a decade a mature umbilicus constricts and pinches off a hollow spore-kite that drifts on the wind until it catches a crown of its own; most fall into the Mire and rot, but the few that strike root found a fleet.

## Myth

Canopy-pilots call a world-tree carrying a dense fleet a "sailed crown" and will not camp beneath one — they say the sails are the tree dreaming of flight, and that to sleep under a dreaming tree is to wake with your feet no longer quite your own. A Cymesail whose tether has just severed is kept as a good-luck rag for a single crossing, then released; to hold one longer is to keep a thing from the one act it was made for.
